Hey Neighbor - Validating the Idea

What Will You Find In This Case Study?

This case study will describe the development and validation of the Hey Neighbor idea. I will go through how I quickly created a realistic prototye using HTML and CSS, then tested it with potential users. Some of the results of this project was the creation of a version one of Hey Neighbors landing page, personas of potential users and a users journey map. To hear the story of my move-in and see how it led me to this project, watch this presentation.

Tools Used:
Illustrator
InDesign

HTML & CSS

What is Hey Neighbor?

People who live in New York City know how difficult it can be to find an apartment. With a city that is over 65% renters, high demand creates a seller's market, resulting in high costs and low quality. My personal experience moving to New York led me to search for a solution. Hey Neighbor is a digital platform that shares insider details about apartments, ensuring NYC renters find comfortable and safe living conditions.

Research and Understanding

After talking with NYC tenants, I recognized the lack of information they had before committing to a new home. Is the super responsive? Will my neighbors party all hours of the night? Just how bad are the cockroaches? Can my landlord really raise the rent that much? The answers to these questions can't be easily found on the web (if at all), and landlords know it. Landlords and the city frequently take advantage of these information deficits and leave tenants to pick up the pieces.
The conversations lead me to ask:

How might more information about specific apartments be provided to tenants before they negotiate and sign expensive contracts?

Market Research:

I initially wanted to see what tools were already on the market for tenants seeking information on apartments. I noticed the websites I found relied on two types of data:

  1. Government-collected data (e.g., code violations or 311 complaints regarding a specific building).
  2. User-generated data (e.g., reviews connected to buildings).

What can we learn from what's already on the market?


Based on research and personal experience with finding an apartment in NYC, I quickly realized that renters need a one-stop web tool that offers practical data and personal reviews.

Creating and Testing the Prototype

I wanted to validate the hypothesis quickly without having to build the functionality of the tool out. With that goal in mind, I created a news article prototype that NYC tenants could quickly read. I edited the HTML on the tech news site Quartz to create a custom web article that could pass as legitimate tech news.

Included with the prototype is an article that featured a solution already on the market (Rentlogic) and compared it to my solution (Hey Neighbor). Rentlogic takes information directly from government sources to create a letter rating (similar to restaurant letter ratings), while Hey Neighbor provides two separate ratings—one based on government data and another on tenant reviews.

You can read more about the differences of the services on the live prototyped article.
Along with this, I mocked up Hey Neighbor’s landing page to help describe the service further if a user clicked through the article to view the home page.

Next, I sat down with potential users and told them they found this article on social media. I then asked them to read the full article while I observed. Some users would click through to one of the service's homepages, but more often we would discuss what their impressions where about what they read.

If they stayed on the article I proceeded to ask a number of questions:

  • Are these services you would use? Why?
  • What kind of value do you see each of these companies bringing?
  • Would you prefer one over the other? Why?
Hey Neighbor Prototype Landing Page (View)
Rentlogic's Landing Page

After a user viewed either Hey Neighbor or Rentlogic’s website, I would ask:

  • Is the website what you expected based on the promises outlined in the article?
  • What features, based on your reading of the article, were missing when you visited the website?
  • After visiting both Hey Neighbor and Rentlogic, which service would you prefer?

Overall Takeaways:

Out of ten interviews, eight preferred Hey Neighbor. Most of the users valued the opinions of tenants that have lived in the same apartment or building. Not only did users want to be able to consume information provided by both the government and other tenants, they valued the social proof that Hey Neighbor promised and Rentlogic lacked.

Comments On Hey Neighbor

“Hey Neighbor feels less corporate. It seems more of a friendlier environment where people can give the real deal on what an apartment is like. It feels like the service wants to help you as if a neighbor/friend was looking out for you.”
— User 3

“If you have to make a snap decision (including renting without actually seeing the apartment) online information is going to be more accessible and thorough (like google rating and yelp) then a grade in a lobby.”
— User 6

“I feel like the actual tenant opinions, stories and reviews in regards to the apartment building, landlords and supers would be very helpful in making the best decision on where I want to call home.”
— User 8

Comments on Rentlogic

“It seems more objective. Reviews are great, but I don't entirely trust other people's opinions. Whereas code violations are more straightforward and less biased.”
— User 1

“I liked the idea of having a grade given to apartment buildings. Instead of having to dig through various reviews, I could quickly get a better picture of how well the apartment is maintained.”
— User 7

My original hypothesis was validated, so I developed a statement to guide the project for the future:

NYC renters need a web tool that gathers and organizes data, personal experience, and reviews on specific buildings and apartments. With information taken from city tracked data-bases and user generated content, Hey Neighbor will improved the decision making of NYC tenants.
